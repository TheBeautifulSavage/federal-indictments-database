## About

Federal indictments are the formal charging documents returned by grand juries authorizing the government to prosecute federal crimes. Tracking federal indictments provides a real-time picture of DOJ prosecution priorities and emerging crime trends.

## The Grand Jury Process

The Fifth Amendment requires federal felony prosecution to begin with grand jury indictment. The grand jury:
- Hears evidence presented by prosecutors
- Meets in secret (grand jury secrecy rule)
- Consists of 16-23 citizens
- Returns indictment if 12+ jurors find probable cause
- Functions as citizen check on government prosecution power

Unlike trial juries, grand juries don't decide guilt — only whether the government has enough evidence to charge.

## Anatomy of a Federal Indictment

Federal indictments include:
- **Caption**: Court, district, case number
- **Counts**: Individual charges, each constituting a separate offense
- **Charging language**: Elements of each offense
- **Forfeiture allegations**: Property subject to forfeiture
- **Signature**: Foreperson of grand jury

Superseding indictments add new charges or defendants to pending cases.

## Sealed Indictments

Federal indictments are often filed under seal when:
- Defendants haven't yet been arrested
- Investigation is ongoing
- Defendants might flee if they knew charges were coming
- Cooperating witnesses need protection

Indictments are unsealed when defendants are arrested or surrender.

## Criminal Complaints

When immediate arrest is needed before grand jury action, prosecutors may file criminal complaints supported by probable cause affidavits. Complaints must be followed by indictment within 30 days (or 21 days for detained defendants) unless defendant waives.
